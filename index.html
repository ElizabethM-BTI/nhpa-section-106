Understanding Program Alternatives — §800.14
What is a "Program Alternative"? Under §800.14, federal agencies and the Council may establish alternatives to the standard case-by-case §800.3–800.6 review process. When an existing program alternative already governs an undertaking, the agency follows that alternative instead of the standard flow — this is the early exit shown at §800.3 in the diagram above. There are five types, each serving a different purpose.

① Alternate Procedures

§800.14(a)

A federal agency develops its own agency-specific procedures to implement Section 106, substituting for all or part of Subpart B. These must be consistent with the Council's regulations and go through a 60-day Council review before adoption. Once approved, they replace the standard §§800.3–800.6 process for that agency.

  • Agency consults with Council, SHPOs/THPOs, tribes, and public during development
  • Published in the Federal Register
  • Cannot override tribal historic preservation regulations on tribal lands

② Programmatic Agreements (PA)

§800.14(b) · §800.16(t)

A PA is a negotiated document between the Council and the agency to govern a program or resolve adverse effects from complex or multiple undertakings. Unlike an MOA (which covers a single undertaking), a PA covers all undertakings within the program until it expires or is terminated.

When a PA may be used:

  • Effects are similar/repetitive or multi-state/regional in scope
  • Effects cannot be fully determined prior to approval of the undertaking
  • Non-federal parties are delegated major decision-making
  • Routine management at federal installations/land-management units
  • Other circumstances warrant departure from the normal process

Signatories: Council + Agency + appropriate SHPO/THPOs (or NCSHPO president for nationwide programs). On tribal lands, THPO or tribal representative must sign.

Prototype PA exception (§800.14(b)(4)): If Council has designated a prototype PA for a type of program, the agency may execute it with the SHPO/THPO only — Council participation and signature not required.

Development process: For complex/multiple undertakings, consultation to develop the PA follows §800.6. Council participation is mandatory (§800.6(a)(1)(i)(C)) — the agency must invite the Council whenever a PA will be the resolution document.

PA vs. MOA:

  • MOA — covers one specific undertaking; effects are determinable; resolves adverse effects on known historic properties
  • PA — covers a program or multiple/complex undertakings; used when effects can't all be determined in advance; compliance with the PA satisfies §106 for all covered undertakings

③ Exempted Categories

§800.14(c)

The Council or an agency may propose that a category of undertakings be entirely exempted from Subpart B review. Requirements for approval:

  • The undertakings would otherwise qualify as "undertakings" under §800.16(y)
  • Potential effects on historic properties are foreseeable and likely to be minimal or not adverse
  • Exemption is consistent with the purposes of the Act

The Council reviews and approves/rejects within 30 days. Once approved, undertakings within that category need no further Subpart B review, unless the agency or Council determines otherwise in a specific case.

④ Standard Treatments

§800.14(d)

The Council may establish standard methods for treating a category of historic properties, undertakings, or effects — e.g., a defined protocol for treating archaeological sites found during construction. Agencies may use these as a basis for an MOA without re-negotiating terms from scratch each time (§800.6(b)(1)(ii)).

  • Council establishes on its own initiative or at request of another party
  • Published in the Federal Register
  • Can be terminated by Council with 30-day notice

⑤ Program Comments

§800.14(e)

An agency may request the Council to comment on an entire category of undertakings rather than conducting individual §§800.4–800.6 reviews for each. The Council responds within 45 days.

  • Agency identifies category, likely effects, and steps it will take to account for them
  • If Council comments: agency takes those comments into account for all undertakings in the category; published in Federal Register
  • If Council declines: agency continues with standard individual §§800.3–800.6 reviews
  • Council may withdraw its program comment if the agency isn't properly taking effects into account

Special Situations: Emergency & Post-Review Discoveries

§800.12 · §800.13

Emergency situations (§800.12): For undertakings responding to a declared disaster or emergency, an agency may comply with §106 through: (1) following an existing PA that includes emergency provisions; or (2) notifying Council, SHPO/THPO, and relevant tribes and affording 7 days to comment (or less if circumstances demand). Applies only to undertakings implemented within 30 days of the declaration.

Post-review discoveries (§800.13): If historic properties are discovered after the §106 process is complete:

  • Best practice: Include discovery procedures in the MOA or PA before work begins
  • If construction has not commenced: consult per §800.6
  • If construction has commenced: notify SHPO/THPO, tribes, and Council within 48 hours; all parties respond within 48 hours; agency takes recommendations into account
  • On tribal lands: also comply with applicable tribal regulations and obtain tribal concurrence
⚡ Linear Energy Infrastructure: How §106 Is Modified
Why linear energy projects are different: Pipelines, transmission lines, and access roads cross hundreds of miles and multiple jurisdictions, making the standard case-by-case §800.3–800.6 review impractical. Agency guidance addresses three recurring challenges: (1) the scope of the "undertaking" when multiple federal authorities touch only parts of a project; (2) the impossibility of completing full survey before authorization when final alignments are unknown; and (3) multi-agency coordination when more than one federal entity has jurisdiction over different segments. The modifications below — phased identification, programmatic agreements, and agency-specific procedures — are all authorized exits or alternatives within the standard 36 C.F.R. Part 800 framework.
FERC

Federal Energy Regulatory Commission — Interstate Natural Gas Pipelines

Natural Gas Act §7(c) · 18 C.F.R. Part 380
⚠ Differs from standard §106 Full route jurisdiction PA common for major projects

Jurisdiction & Undertaking Scope

FERC must authorize the entire route of an interstate natural gas pipeline under Natural Gas Act §7(c). Accordingly, FERC's §106 undertaking is the complete pipeline, not discrete segments — unlike USACE, which has jurisdiction only over water crossings. FERC remains legally responsible for all §106 findings and determinations even when a project sponsor performs the fieldwork.

Project Sponsor Role (§800.2(a)(3))

FERC authorizes project sponsors (the pipeline company) to initiate contact with consulting parties and to prepare all cultural resource reports. Sponsors assist FERC; FERC staff makes every formal determination. Because FERC is a quasi-judicial body bound by the Administrative Procedure Act, off-the-record communications with FERC staff are prohibited — all submissions go through the formal docket.

Two Application Tracks

  • Pre-filing environmental review (required for LNG projects; voluntary for others): Sponsor initiates §106 outreach and fieldwork months before the formal application, giving tribes and SHPOs early notice and time to consult
  • Direct application: Sponsor files the application and the pre-construction §106 process runs concurrent with FERC review

APE Definition for Natural Gas Projects

  • Direct APE: All construction areas — ROW, compressor/meter stations, staging areas, extra-work spaces, access roads, ancillary facilities. The same project may carry different APEs for direct (ground-disturbing) effects vs. indirect (visual, audible, vibratory, atmospheric) effects on aboveground resources
  • Survey corridor: Should be wide enough to accommodate minor route modifications so that route adjustments can still avoid or minimize impacts on resources already identified
  • Indirect APE: Extends beyond the construction footprint for viewshed, noise, and vibration effects on historic structures and landscapes

Boring/Directional Drilling — Avoidance vs. Mitigation (Critical Distinction)

FERC draws a bright-line rule determining whether boring under a historic property is avoidance (no treatment plan required) or mitigation (treatment plan required):

  • Avoidance — no treatment plan when: (1) the pipeline is at least 25 feet from any portion of the historic property, AND (2) no construction, operation, or maintenance will occur within the historic property boundaries. Required docs: bore pit/lay-down area locations, scaled plan/profile drawing, access routes that avoid the property, pre-construction geotechnical studies
  • Mitigation — treatment plan required when either criterion above cannot be met. The treatment plan must include: a contingency plan if boring fails, pre-boring documentation of aboveground features, and long-term maintenance access protocols

Tribal Outreach — Broader Than Standard §106

Sponsors must identify and consult all tribes with current or historical use of or occupancy in the project area — including tribes no longer residing in the region. This is broader than standard §106, which focuses on tribes with demonstrated concern for properties in the APE.

Required Reports (Sequential)

  • Overview Report — background research, existing resource data, proposed APE, identification of consulting parties
  • Survey Report — field methods, resources identified, NRHP eligibility recommendations
  • Evaluation Report — formal NRHP eligibility determinations; if no adverse effect, process ends here
  • Agreement Document — FERC drafts the MOA or PA; project sponsor does not draft it
  • Treatment Plan — mitigation specifics for each adversely affected historic property
  • Management Summary — due within 30 days of completing fieldwork
  • Treatment Report — due within 1 year of completing fieldwork; documents all mitigation completed

Plan for Unanticipated Cultural Resources

Required as part of every application: specifies stop-work procedures if unanticipated resources are discovered during construction. Must address human remains separately under NAGPRA.

Blanket Certificate Program

Minor modifications at existing natural gas facilities (e.g., adding a compressor unit) may qualify for a blanket certificate — a pre-authorized, streamlined FERC process with simplified §106 review rather than the full report sequence above.

USACE

U.S. Army Corps of Engineers — Pipeline Water Crossings & Corps Land

Clean Water Act §404 · Rivers & Harbors Act §10 · MLA §28
⚠ Segmented review (contested) ACHP dispute over undertaking scope NWP 12 for most crossings

Narrow Jurisdiction: Water Crossings Only

The Corps has no authority to approve pipelines along their entire domestic routes. Its jurisdiction is limited to: (1) activities in §404 waters and wetlands; (2) structures/work in §10 navigable waters; and (3) crossings of Corps-managed civil works lands (requiring a §408 permission and easement). Upland pipeline segments on private land are entirely outside Corps jurisdiction and therefore outside Corps §106 review.

Corps' Own §106 Procedures (Appendix C to 33 C.F.R. Part 325)

The Corps follows its own "Procedures for the Protection of Historic Properties" in Appendix C — not the standard 36 C.F.R. Part 800. As of 2016, ACHP has not accepted the Corps' Appendix C as consistent with ACHP regulations (which require ACHP to approve any alternate procedures under §800.14(a)). Some federal district courts have declined to apply Appendix C on grounds of inconsistency with NHPA; others have upheld it. This institutional dispute remains unresolved.

The "Small Federal Handle" Problem

When a major pipeline's only federal touch-points are discrete water crossings, the Corps treats each crossing as a separate undertaking. Critics (including ACHP) argue this fails to assess cumulative effects on historic properties across the entire pipeline corridor. ACHP has formally objected, stating the Corps' approach "dismisses the potential for effects to historic properties that may be located within the broader project area of an undertaking when properly defined." Courts have reached inconsistent conclusions on this dispute.

Nationwide Permit 12 (NWP 12) — Used for Most Pipeline Crossings

  • Pre-authorizes utility-line activities (including oil and gas pipelines) in waters of the U.S. where the activity will not result in loss of more than 0.5 acres of waters
  • General Condition 20 (Historic Properties): No NWP activity may be authorized if it may affect NRHP-eligible properties, until §106 consultation is complete
  • GC 17 (Tribal Rights): No NWP activity may cause more than minimal adverse effects on tribal rights, including treaty rights
  • Individual NWP 12 verifications do NOT require NEPA documentation or a public comment period — public notice happened at the rulemaking stage

Pre-Construction Notification (PCN) and §106 Trigger

For NWP activities, the project applicant — not the Corps — identifies historic properties and submits a PCN. §106 review is triggered only when the applicant raises it in the PCN. Corps then verifies within 45 days (extended if ESA §7 or NHPA §106 consultation is needed). For activities below NWP thresholds that don't require a PCN, applicants self-certify compliance without Corps review.

Individual Permits — Standard §106 Applies

Proposed crossings exceeding NWP 12 thresholds (e.g., >0.5 acre wetland loss) require an individual permit. For individual permits, the Corps district engineer is responsible for identifying historic properties and completing consultations — following standard §800.4–800.6 procedures. Individual permits require public notice, an alternatives analysis, and site-specific NEPA documentation.

Civil Works Easements (Corps Land Crossings)

When a pipeline crosses Corps-managed federal land or a Corps flood easement on private land, the developer needs both a §408 permission (to alter the civil works project) and a real estate easement (Mineral Leasing Act §28). As part of the §408 technical review, the Corps evaluates NEPA and §106 compliance — here the "undertaking" is the crossing of Corps land, with a geographic scope broader than a simple NWP verification.

DAPL as Context

The Dakota Access Pipeline controversy illustrates these tensions: NWP 12 was used for 202 water crossings; no individual §106 consultation was required for the upland route; the easement for the Lake Oahe crossing — 0.55 miles from the Standing Rock Sioux reservation — became the focal point of tribal legal challenges asserting inadequate §106 consultation.

BLM

Bureau of Land Management — Linear Projects on Federal Land (PA Framework)

FLPMA · BLM National PA · 36 C.F.R. §800.14(b)
Phased PA approach Permittee funds fieldwork Annual cycle replaces case-by-case review

BLM National Programmatic Agreement (nPA)

BLM does not follow the standard §800.3–800.6 process for routine undertakings. Instead, the BLM National PA (executed with ACHP and NCSHPO; current version amended 2024) governs all BLM §106 compliance through state-specific BLM-SHPO protocols. Each state BLM office maintains a protocol with at least 17 required elements that streamline identification, evaluation, and resolution for routine undertakings. Annual reports track implementation.

Project-Specific PA for Complex Linear Projects (§800.14(b))

For large linear projects where effects cannot be fully determined before authorization (e.g., a 211-mile industrial road, a pipeline corridor crossing diverse terrain), BLM executes a project-specific PA that establishes a phased alternative to §§800.3–800.6. This PA is developed through §800.6 consultation and Council participation is mandatory (§800.6(a)(1)(i)(C)) because a PA will be the resolution document.

NEPA–NHPA Coordination Requirement

Per BLM Instruction Memorandum 2012-108: BLM must complete both NHPA §106 and tribal consultation prior to making a final decision. A categorical exclusion under NEPA does not satisfy §106 — both processes run concurrently but separately. BLM uses a side-by-side process chart to track parallel NEPA and §106 milestones.

Permittee Responsibilities

The project developer (permittee) funds and implements all cultural resource work — survey, evaluation, treatment — under BLM direction. No ground disturbance may occur in any project segment until BLM provides written notice that §106 requirements are satisfied for that segment.

GIS Predictive Modeling & Risk-Based Survey

For multi-hundred-mile corridors, 100% pedestrian survey is impractical pre-authorization. BLM's phased PA approach uses:

  • GIS archaeological potential model — categorizes the entire APE as High, Medium, or Low potential based on landforms, known site locations, water features, vegetation, and other variables
  • Differentiated survey coverage: 100% of High potential areas; 50% of Medium; 10% of Low (within the direct APE)
  • Model refined annually as new fieldwork data are collected
  • Indirect APE: Subsurface survey not required unless reasonably foreseeable adverse effects; only surface resources addressed

Alternative Four Step Process — Annual Cycle

The phased PA replaces the single-event §800.4–800.6 review with an annual cycle tied to construction phases and project segments:

  • Annual Work Plan (submitted by March 1 or 60 days before first fieldwork) — Survey strategy, GIS model updates, schedule; approved by BLM and SHPO before implementation
  • Interim Report — Indirect APE (within 30 days post-fieldwork) — Identifies Indirect APE resources; 15-day consulting party review to identify which warrant evaluation
  • Annual Fieldwork Report (within 90 days post-fieldwork) — NRHP eligibility recommendations, effect findings, proposed mitigation; 45-day consulting party review; BLM submits DOEs to SHPO within 15 days
  • Treatment Plans (within 120 days of SHPO acceptance of DOEs) — Property-specific mitigation plans; 30-day review; approved by BLM and SHPO
  • Final Implementation Report (within 180 days of treatment completion) — Comprehensive record; BLM approval = §106 satisfied for that historic property

Multi-Agency Coordination

BLM designates itself as lead federal agency under §800.2(a)(2). Other agencies with jurisdiction over portions of the corridor (NPS for national preserve crossings; USACE for water crossings; State agencies for state land) sign as invited signatories and enforce the PA within their own jurisdiction. Each must provide written notice to BLM when §106 is satisfied within their scope before BLM authorizes construction in that segment.

Long-Term Considerations

  • PA term: Up to 25 years (Ambler Road PA example, 2020–2045)
  • NRHP eligibility re-evaluation every 5 years or following significant design changes
  • Re-survey required if >10 years have passed since original survey and design changes affect surveyed areas
  • Broad-scale cumulative effects addressed within 3 years of completing initial inventory

Key Definitions — §800.16
Undertaking §800.16(y) Any project, activity, or program funded in whole or in part under the direct or indirect jurisdiction of a federal agency — including those carried out by or on behalf of a federal agency; those carried out with federal financial assistance; and those requiring a federal permit, license, or approval.
Historic Property §800.16(l) Any prehistoric or historic district, site, building, structure, or object included in, or eligible for inclusion in, the National Register of Historic Places. Includes artifacts and records related to and within such properties. Also includes properties of traditional religious and cultural importance to an Indian tribe or NHO that meet NR criteria.
Effect §800.16(i) Alteration to the characteristics of a historic property qualifying it for inclusion in or eligibility for the National Register.
Area of Potential Effects (APE) §800.16(d) The geographic area(s) within which an undertaking may directly or indirectly cause alterations in the character or use of historic properties. May be different for different kinds of effects from the same undertaking.
Consultation §800.16(f) The process of seeking, discussing, and considering the views of other participants, and, where feasible, seeking agreement with them regarding matters arising in the §106 process. Government-to-government consultation with Indian tribes required.
Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) §800.16(o) The document recording the terms and conditions agreed upon to resolve the adverse effects of an individual undertaking upon historic properties. Governs the undertaking and all its parts. Executed and filed with the Council.
Programmatic Agreement (PA) §800.16(t) A document recording terms and conditions to resolve the potential adverse effects of a federal agency program, complex undertaking, or other situations per §800.14(b). Covers all undertakings within the program — satisfies §106 for each one until the PA expires or is terminated.
Foreclosure §800.16(j) An agency action that effectively precludes the Council from providing comments that the agency official can meaningfully consider prior to approval. Prohibited — §106 review must be completed before approval of expenditure of federal funds or issuance of a license.
Comment §800.16(e) The findings and recommendations of the Council formally provided in writing to the head of a federal agency under §106. Distinct from the Council's advisory opinions during earlier stages of the process.
SHPO §800.16(v) State Historic Preservation Officer — the official appointed under §101(b)(1) to administer the state historic preservation program. Primary consulting party for most undertakings.
THPO §800.16(w) Tribal Historic Preservation Officer — tribal official who has assumed the SHPO's responsibilities for §106 compliance on tribal lands under §101(d)(2). Acts in lieu of the SHPO for undertakings on tribal lands.
National Historic Landmark (NHL) §800.16(p) A historic property designated by the Secretary of the Interior as a National Historic Landmark. Undertakings adversely affecting NHLs require mandatory Council participation and Secretary notification (§800.10).
